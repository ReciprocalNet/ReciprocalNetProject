A candle is often used as a light source with a wick in the center that rises from a column of fuel. The fuel used today is exclusively some sort of wax, paraffin wax being the most common. Soy and vegetable candles are also available. Often candles are scented with aromatic oils such as cinnamon, limonene, vanilla, or musk fragrances, which are used to freshen the scent of the home. The wick is saturated with solid fuel and the heat of the match first melts and then vaporizes a small amount of the fuel. This fuel then combines with the oxygen in the air to form a flame. The heat from the flame melts the candle as the fuel moves up the wick via capillary action and the candle continues to burn while growing shorter. The flame appears bluer in the hotter regions where the hydrogen is burned to form water vapor. In the brighter, more yellow part of the flame carbon soot is oxidized to form carbon dioxide. Before the invention of electricity, the candle was a common source of lighting.
